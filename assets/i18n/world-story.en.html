Even before time existed, they were already in the world.

The Dryad, who was the first, sank into an eternal sleep of silence.

Her loyal Keeper remained awake, but he had watched for too long…

Perhaps he remembers everything, but now his speech and thoughts have drifted so far that no living being can fully understand them anymore.

Mr. Dream remembers the beginning—the moment from which the Big Bang unfolded.

But the span between that moment and the appearance of the first human has been erased, and that memory slips from his mind whenever he tries to recall it. His line of stable memory began with the appearance of the first Thread. No one knows who or what wove these Threads. Yet almost every sentient being on Earth has its Thread—except those who live in the world of the Great Dryad (perhaps—though more likely not "perhaps," but certainly—they have Threads too, but not in this world). The essence, the foundation of this world: each Thread in the Dryad's world is a reflection of each person on Earth.

Dream fully identified himself with one purpose—to serve the Threads.

His task is the multitude of Threads he must guard.

Threads do not show who you will become, how much you will earn, or how you will die; it would be too much to call them fate. They do not predict the future. They simply grow, and Dream simply knows what a Thread is meant to grow into—and like a gardener in love with his plants, he tends them in the garden. So Dream watches the Threads as they grow, tangle with one another, sometimes try to grow into something they were never meant to be.

Then Dream's team helps return a Thread to its path—sometimes by guiding, sometimes by trimming. A team, because when there were more Threads, other beings joined Dream: as strange as he, as forgetful of their past as he.

But not all Threads were in Dream's Garden.

There was another place—the Wasteland, where no one lived, though there were more Threads there than anywhere else. There were so many that from afar they looked like one solid mass.

The Wasteland was more terrifying than death and more terrifying than evil. Evil usually has a purpose; death ends everything; even pain is still a feeling. In the Wasteland there was nothing but this tangled mass of empty threads. These Threads belonged to people who did not break, did not scream, did not reach for the light and did not ask for darkness. They simply existed. Their human reflections on Earth could speak, work, bear children, build houses, buy things, laugh in the right places, and die on the appointed day. But their Threads remained empty lines without inner movement.

Dream, of course, knew of the Wasteland. But he did not want to go there—frankly, he was simply afraid. He loved working with Threads, supporting them, guiding them, helping them cope with Thirst—but in the Threads of the Wasteland there was nothing that needed to be returned to a path.

This was a world of stagnation and order; there was no time here. Nothing changed except the Threads. The world was perfect in its stable, motionless flux—until the first cry, unfitting amid the eternal whisper, tore through the silence of the roots. The appearance of her who could change on her own, and who taught the Threads to change, became the beginning of the end of eternity.
