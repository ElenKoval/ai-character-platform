Sometimes Pak was lucky. He would notice an anomaly in a Thread while still in the Garden, and then there was no need to take on human form and descend below. It wasn't that he disliked humans; it was just that here, among the interweaving of world energies, maintaining Order was much simpler. More sterile.

Today was exactly that kind of day.

One Thread began to loop. Instead of carrying the flow of energy toward its goal, it coiled into an ugly knot, consuming light. From Pak's perspective, it was a pure parasite. A bug in the code of the universe. A waste of resource.

"Dream will say again: 'Let us observe'... But the Garden requires precision," Pak rumbled, and a scalpel flashed in his claws, long and sharp as a shard of a fallen star. One precise cut, and the line of fate would become straight again.

Liara entered the Garden silently, only the rustle of her scales betraying her presence. She didn't try to stop him. She simply held out an apple.

For a dragon's paw, it was tiny and looked ridiculous. It was ugly, with one side bloated and a deep scar from some ancient drought.

"Is it spoiled?" she asked, her vertical pupils narrowing.

"It is defective," Pak cut her off, his voice echoing like low thunder against the stones. "Inefficient. It violates the geometry of the branch and the logic of ripening."

Liara silently bit exactly where the roughest, sun-scorched skin was. She squinted.

"It is concentrated," she exhaled along with a cloud of light steam. "It fought for every drop of water. There is more fury and flavor in this than in ten of your perfect spheres."

Pak froze. The sound of his breathing became heavy and rhythmic.

Order demanded the immediate excision of "garbage." But Liara stood too close. She smelled of that apple: a sharp, wild, living aroma that did not fit into his blueprints of an ideal world.

He slowly lowered the scalpel.

Pak took the apple, almost hiding it in his palm, disdainfully wiped it against the edge of his flawless wing, and tucked it away.

"The Thread remains," he said coldly, straightening his shoulders. "As a deviant case. For the study of the system's limit of degradation."

He never called it mercy. He called it "data collection."
