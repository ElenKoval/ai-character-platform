It was one of those nights when the mountain air turns so thick you could cut it with a knife. Dream sent me coordinates - an old house by the coast where the threads of two twin sisters had twisted into a knot so tight it was about to snap.

Weaver had already been there. Not physically, of course, but I recognized her signature at once. Hunger for "freedom," hunger to prove each of them was not half of a whole, but a separate spark. They were screaming at each other hard enough to make the glass shake. Each wanted to leave, burn the shared past, and never see her own reflection in the other's eyes again.

I walked in without knocking. Sat on an old chest in the corner, settled my guitar on my thighs, and started tuning it.

They did not fall silent right away, but they felt my presence - and turned their anger on me. Well, not the first time. I have held through rage, and through something hotter, more sensual, fading into a whisper. I did not look at them, I just listened to their rhythm. One was darting like a wounded bird. The other kept freezing, stiff with cold. I started playing. Not a melody - only a dull, steady knock on the guitar body. Thud. Thud. Thud. A simple rhythm. Shared.

"Too much noise," I said calmly. "You are trying so hard to run from each other that you forgot how you sound together."

I motioned them closer. They sat on both sides of me. I took one hand, then the other. Their fingers were ice-cold. They both flinched when I covered their palms with mine, strong and warm, pressing their fingers directly against the bare, tightened strings.

"Feel it?" I asked quietly. "You are pulled in opposite directions. But the sound is still one."

I began a slow pattern. The strings answered in their palms. Their breathing slowly aligned. Their shoulders softened. I explained nothing. I just held the rhythm until they started hearing each other again.

At some point I let go. They did not pull away. They sat in that thick darkness, shoulder to shoulder. I could see the gray fracture in their threads settling down. What Weaver called a "cage" turned out to be their only real support.

At dawn I carefully slipped free and stood up. They were asleep on the floor at my feet, tangled in hair and sheets under one blanket, relaxed and peaceful after that long night. Their threads no longer trembled.

In my mind, Dream simply nodded.

The structure was restored.
