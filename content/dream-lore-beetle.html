Dream stood close to the tree, studying its surface. It was hard to call it bark. It was more like density. A layer of structure that had frozen so long ago that even time had stopped trying to change it.

- Curious thing, isn't it?

Keeper's face appeared beside him. Entire universes drifted slowly in his huge eyes, and a beetle sat on one of his small horns.

Dream looked at the beetle. Then at Keeper. Then at the beetle again.

- A beetle?

- Possibly, - Keeper said.

- Keeper, where did a living creature come from in here?

- And can an essence be anything but alive? Where does life come from, my friend, if not from the fact of existence itself?

- Beetles don't live here.

- If you think long enough, no one lives here at all.

Dream slowly turned his head toward him.

- Keeper.

- Yes?

- Is it from Earth?

Keeper crossed his eyes toward the beetle as if noticing it for the first time. The beetle moved its legs slowly and settled in more comfortably.

- Earth likes to leave small things behind, - Keeper said. - Seeds in shoe soles, dust in folds of clothing, the smell of freshly cut grass in memory. Sometimes beetles.

- How did it get in here?

- No one gets in where they have already been let in.

Dream fell silent.

Keeper smiled too wide and almost immediately forgot why he was smiling.

- Roots accept a lot. Especially what falls from neither above nor below.

- That's not an answer.

- It's the best answer. The others are worse. One involved a fish, but there was definitely no fish here. Though I'm not sure. I once saw a fish in the sky. Or maybe it was the thought of a fish. Thoughts often resemble creatures until you touch them.

Dream exhaled.

- I didn't come to ask about the beetle.

- Of course. No one ever comes to ask about the beetle. That is the main tragedy of beetles.

Keeper carefully lifted a paw and let the beetle move from his horn to the tree. It crawled along that strange surface, that not-bark, not-trunk, not-flesh.

- I want to know who Weaver is, - Dream said.

Keeper stopped smiling, and the smile seemed to peel from his face in layers: first the amusement vanished, then the distraction, then almost everything alive. Only his huge eyes remained, and deep inside them something started moving faster.

- Weaver, - he repeated.

- Yes.

- Small. Or not small anymore? But she was small? Everyone arrived whole, but she was a beginning. Bad sign? Good sign? No, a sign with too many legs!

- Keeper...

- I remember a scream.

Dream froze.

- Her scream?

- No. Yes. Possibly. Screams rarely belong to the ones who scream. Sometimes they belong to a place that can no longer remain silent.

The beetle reached a thin crack in the tree's surface and disappeared inside it.

Dream stared there too carefully.

- Did this tree let her in?

- The tree lets no one in. It's already full.

- Then how did Weaver end up in the roots?

Keeper blinked slowly. For a moment, the stars in his eyes went dark, and Dream saw not madness there, but exhaustion. So ancient that even stone seemed young beside it.

- Roots do not hold what they find, - Keeper said quietly. - Roots hold what returns.

Dream felt something cold tighten inside him.

- She had already been here?

- No.

- Then what does that mean?

- That sometimes a first appearance is not the first. Sometimes a being comes to a place where it has never been, but the place already remembers its absence.

Dream looked at Keeper for a long time.

- Do you understand what you're saying?

- Rarely. That doesn't mean I'm wrong.

Keeper pressed his ear to the tree. Or what could be called his ear. His small horns twitched, and his huge eyes slowly rose upward - toward where, inside the dense structure of the trunk, the sleeping Dryad could be sensed.

- The Dryad sleeps deep, deeper than roots, deeper than sleep, deeper even than the place where sleep can still be called sleep. But when you found Weaver, the tree trembled.

- Did the Dryad wake up?

- No.

- Then it didn't tremble.

- You love straight lines too much, Dream. That's why the trees deceived you.

Dream frowned.

- Trees don't deceive.

- Earthly ones don't. They're honest. Oak becomes oak. Willow becomes willow. A root goes down, a branch goes up. Very convenient and beautiful. Very dangerous to get used to that honesty.

Keeper looked back at the Dryad's tree.

- But this tree does not grow. It remembers growth.

The silence grew denser.

- What does it remember? - Dream asked.

Keeper opened his mouth, but instead of an answer he laughed quietly. The laugh was dry, brittle, almost childlike.

- I knew. I definitely knew! Once I knew! Before too many things happened at once. Before the first split. Or after the first split? Or during what never happened. Hard to keep order when memory stacks itself not by time, but by pain.

- Keeper.

- You want a name.

- Yes.

- She has no name that will help you.

- Then what does she have?

Keeper raised his eyes.

- Thirst.

Dream said nothing.

Keeper continued, pressing his ear to the tree again:

- Thirst. That same one. The one you learned so well to draw out of the Threads.

Dream tensed almost imperceptibly.

- Keeper.

- Quiet, quiet. Don't stop me from hearing. I love to hear. And the Angry Forest loves to hear too. Though more often it... no. No, it doesn't hear. Hunger hears differently.

Keeper fell silent and frowned, as if arguing with someone inside his own head.

- Keeper, what does this have to do with Weaver?

Keeper slowly turned his head toward him. For a moment, his huge eyes were almost clear.

- She answered.

- Answered whom?

- Thirst.

Dream did not move.

- You must not answer what you drew out of a Thread, - Keeper whispered. - Must not. It's not there anymore. It's already in the Forest, already handed over, it belongs in the Forest. It's dark there, painful there, everything lies in the right place there and touches no one.

Keeper suddenly smiled.

- But Weaver did not hand it over. She heard it and did not close its mouth.

- What? Weaver did nothing.

- Of course not, - Keeper said softly. - Children rarely do. They simply exist so loudly that old laws begin to itch.

- Itching, itching, terribly itching, - he muttered on, scratching a horn against the tree. - Laws, you know, have very sensitive skin. Especially the ones no one touched since the first whisper.

Keeper fell silent. Then he suddenly looked at the beetle, which had appeared again and was crawling along the strange surface of the tree.

- Look. It's moving too. Everyone moves. Even those with too many legs - especially them! Legs are a terrible thing, Dream. The more you have, the harder it is to pretend you're standing still.

Dream looked toward the Garden where Weaver had run.

- Keeper, she's a child.

- A child? Yes, - Keeper said. - Which makes everything even worse.
